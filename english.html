Overview of the Finance Bill, 2025

The Finance Bill, 2025 is a legislative proposal introduced to amend various tax and fiscal laws in Kenya. It seeks to adjust provisions under existing Acts such as the Income Tax Act (Cap. 470), the Value Added Tax Act (No. 35 of 2013), the Excise Duty Act (No. 23 of 2015), and others. Its overall aim is to enhance government revenue, address administrative gaps, and align the tax system with current economic realities.

The Bill is set to commence on 1st July 2025, except for Sections 12 and 56, which become effective from 1st January 2026 (Clause 1, p. 1).

Civic Education and FAQ

Understanding the Finance Bill is key to grasping how Kenya funds its government and drives economic growth. The Finance Bill 2025 proposes tax and policy changes to support a budget of KES 3.914 trillion, aiming to reduce public debt and fund essential services.

What is a Finance Bill?

A Finance Bill is a legislative proposal introduced annually by the Kenyan government, typically through the National Treasury, to outline revenue-raising measures such as taxes, levies, and fees. It amends existing tax laws (e.g., Income Tax Act, Value Added Tax Act) to fund the national budget, pay off public debt, and support development projects like education, healthcare, and infrastructure.

How is the Finance Bill Used?

The Finance Bill funds the government’s operations and priorities. It determines how much revenue will be collected through taxes (e.g., income tax, VAT, excise duties) and appropriations-in-aid, then allocates these funds to ministries, counties, and programs. For 2025, the bill targets KES 3.385 trillion in revenue to address a fiscal deficit of KES 703.9 billion, or 3.9% of GDP, balancing borrowing and spending on public services.

How Does the Finance Bill Run the Kenyan Government?

The process starts with the National Treasury drafting the bill, guided by the Budget Policy Statement and public input. It’s presented to the National Assembly (e.g., on April 30, 2025, for the 2025 bill), undergoes public participation as required by Article 118 of the Constitution, and faces debate and amendments. Once passed and assented to by the President, it becomes the Finance Act, enabling the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) to collect taxes and the government to finance operations, from paying teachers to building roads.

Examples from Previous Finance Bills

  • Finance Bill 2023: Introduced a 3% housing levy on monthly salaries to fund affordable housing, raised VAT on petroleum products from 8% to 16%, and imposed a 3% tax on digital assets to boost revenue. These changes aimed to address financial inclusion and economic empowerment but sparked debate over cost of living.
  • Finance Bill 2024: Proposed a 16% VAT on bread and a 2.5% motor vehicle tax, but public protests led to their removal. It aimed to raise KES 346 billion, but President Ruto rejected it on June 28, 2024, after unrest, opting for a KES 999 billion budget cut instead.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

The Finance Bill 2025 aims to widen the tax base to collect KES 3.385 trillion in revenue to fund a KES 3.914 trillion budget, reduce public debt (currently 68% of GDP), and limit borrowing. New taxes, like expanded VAT and a five-year cap on carrying forward tax losses, help close revenue gaps amid global trade challenges, such as U.S. tariffs affecting Kenyan exports.

Public participation is mandated by the Constitution. The Finance and National Planning Committee calls for written and oral submissions after the bill is tabled (e.g., April 30, 2025, for the 2025 bill). Attend hearings, submit feedback via email or memoranda, or follow updates from the National Assembly or media like the Daily Nation to voice concerns before the July 1, 2025, effective date.

If the President doesn’t assent, as with the Finance Bill 2024, the government reverts to the previous Finance Act (e.g., 2023) for tax collection. This may lead to budget cuts (e.g., KES 999 billion in 2024), delaying projects like roads or healthcare, and increased borrowing to cover the deficit, impacting essential services.

Proposals to shift essential goods from zero-rated to 16% VAT aim to boost revenue but have faced opposition for raising living costs. Stakeholders argue this burdens Kenyans, and public hearings (starting July 2, 2025, across ten counties) allow feedback to potentially revise such measures before finalization.
