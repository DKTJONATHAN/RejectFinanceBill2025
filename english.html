Reject Finance Bill 2025

Overview of the Finance Bill, 2025

The Finance Bill, 2025 is a legislative proposal introduced to amend various tax and fiscal laws in Kenya. It seeks to adjust provisions under existing Acts such as the Income Tax Act (Cap. 470), the Value Added Tax Act (No. 35 of 2013), the Excise Duty Act (No. 23 of 2015), and others. Its overall aim is to enhance government revenue, address administrative gaps, and align the tax system with current economic realities.

The Bill is set to commence on 1st July 2025, except for Sections 12 and 56, which become effective from 1st January 2026 (Clause 1, p. 1).

20 Reasons Why the Bill May Be Considered Harmful to the Common Mwananchi

1

Higher Motor Vehicle Tax

Imposes an annual tax of 2.5% of vehicle value, with a minimum of KES 5,000 and no upper cap, likely raising transport costs for all Kenyans (Clause 51, p. 192).

2

VAT on Bread

Bread will now attract VAT, increasing the cost of this staple food (First Schedule amendments, VAT Act, p. 140–141).

3

Tax on Financial Services

The exemption for financial services is removed, making bank charges and loan access more expensive (Clause 31, p. 117).

4

Excise Duty on M-PESA and Airtime

Excise duty increases from 15% to 20% on telephone and internet data services and money transfer fees, increasing communication and mobile banking costs (Clause 37, p. 149).

5

Excise on Cement and Construction Inputs

Higher excise on construction materials will likely make housing more expensive (Clause 37, p. 151).

6

Withholding Tax on Digital Services

Expands withholding tax obligations to individuals earning via digital platforms, reducing net income (Clause 8, p. 18).

7

Expanded Definition of Digital Content Monetisation

Targets online creators and freelancers with turnover tax and digital income tax (Clause 6, p. 13–14).

8

Increased Import Declaration and Export Promotion Levies

Raises costs for imported goods and materials (Clause 44 & 45, p. 183–184).

9

Removal of VAT Exemption on Fertilizers and Pesticides

Could raise the cost of agricultural inputs, affecting food prices (Clause 30, p. 111).

10

Restriction of Tax Reliefs

Repeal of various tax reliefs such as for insurance and home ownership reduces disposable income (Clause 10, p. 31–34).

11

Introduction of Eco Levy on Locally Manufactured Plastic Items

Though environmental, this will raise consumer prices on many common items (Clause 46, p. 185).

12

Taxation of Sacco Interest Income

Repeals VAT exemption on Sacco services, impacting low-income savers (Clause 31, p. 117).

13

Removal of PAYE Band Relief

While not stated explicitly, restructuring could shrink net pay for salaried workers (Clause 9, p. 21).

14

Digital Marketplace Definition Broadening

Captures more low-income earners under tax brackets previously exempt (Clause 2, p. 5).

15

No Relief for Minimum Top-Up Tax

Multinational compliance burden may be passed to local consumers (Clause 7, p. 17).

16

Excise on Sanitary Products and Diapers

Repeal of exemptions could raise costs for essential hygiene products (Clause 37, p. 147).

17

New Tax on Motorcycles

Will raise boda boda costs, which are widely used by the common mwananchi (Clause 37, p. 150).

18

Reduced Access to Affordable Loans

Taxation on digital and financial services may deter micro-lending platforms (Clause 31, p. 117).

19

Exporters Lose Duty Remission Benefits

Removal of zero-rated status on certain export supplies discourages local producers (Clause 30, p. 111).

20

Excise on Bottled Water

Raises daily consumption costs for many households (Clause 37, p. 147).
